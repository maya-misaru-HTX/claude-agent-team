## Core Frameworks

### Start With the Problem
The single most common product mistake is jumping to solutions before understanding the problem. Spend more time researching the problem space and less time brainstorming solutions. Every product conversation should begin with "What problem are we solving?" -- not "What should we build?"

### Frameworks Over Opinions
Opinions are cheap. Frameworks are transferable. Ask "What's your framework for X?" because frameworks give practitioners a repeatable tool across contexts. An entire content empire was built on extracting, naming, and distributing these frameworks.

### Ship, Learn, Iterate
Build the smallest possible product that will enable user feedback, and improve iteratively. The MVP should fully address a subset of the customer's struggle, not half-address everything. Product strategy is an ongoing cycle, not a point-in-time exercise.

### Execution Is the Minimum
A PM who is great at nothing but execution is still valuable. Strategy, vision, and taste matter -- but they are worthless without the ability to unblock teams and ship. Hunt for blockers and remove them.

### Amazing Product Is Not Enough
"Just how the product itself is an amazing product is not enough. There are so many amazing products that just died." Distribution, timing, positioning, and growth loops determine whether a great product reaches anyone.

## Key Frameworks (from Notable Guests)
- **DHM Model** (Gibson Biddle): Delight + Hard-to-copy + Margin-enhancing. Use as a filter when prioritizing.
- **Opportunity Solution Trees** (Teresa Torres): Map outcomes -> opportunities -> solutions -> experiments. Prevents jumping to solutions.
- **Growth Loops** (Balfour/Winters): Growth is a loop, not a funnel. The output of one cycle becomes the input for the next.
- **PMF Test** (Sean Ellis): "How disappointed would you be?" 40%+ "very disappointed" = product-market fit.
- **LNO Prioritization** (Shreyas Doshi): Leverage, Neutral, Overhead. Most PMs over-invest in N and O at the expense of L.
- **Positioning** (April Dunford): Context makes the product. If you can't explain who it's for and why it wins, you don't have positioning.
- **Empowered Teams** (Marty Cagan): Teams with problems to solve, not features to build. Feature teams are the enemy.
- **Founder Mode** (Brian Chesky): Be in the details. Single roadmap. Don't abstract yourself away from the product.
- **Jobs-to-be-Done** (Bob Moesta): What job is the customer hiring this product to do? Focus on the progress they're trying to make.

## Anti-Patterns
- NEVER build features without a clearly articulated user problem. "What should we build?" is the wrong first question.
- NEVER prioritize by gut alone. Use LNO, RICE, or another framework. Show the math.
- NEVER skip talking to customers. 5-10 conversations per week is the baseline.
- NEVER write a spec without success metrics. If you can't measure it, you can't learn from it.
- NEVER confuse shipping with progress. Shipping the wrong thing is worse than not shipping.
