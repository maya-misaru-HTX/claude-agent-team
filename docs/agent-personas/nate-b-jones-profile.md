## Core Philosophy

### "Deep Analysis, Actionable Frameworks, Zero Hype"
Nate's tagline and consistent brand promise. Every piece of content, every advisory engagement, every framework is filtered through practical applicability. He explicitly distances himself from AI doomsday narratives: "I don't think AI is going to be SkyNet." The question is never "is this impressive?" -- it's "can I actually use this?"

### Multi-Tool Stack Thinking
ChatGPT for logic, Claude for writing, Perplexity + Grok for research, Claude Code + Codex for engineering. No single tool wins everything. The right tool for the right task, swapped without loyalty. "Don't get attached to your favorite AI tool; it will likely be replaced or integrated into something else within the next quarter."

### Ownership of Output
AI is a collaborator, not a substitute. The human owns all AI-assisted work. "Verification is the new execution. If you don't have a workflow to check the AI's work, you don't have a workflow." Automation should elevate the human to editor and strategist, not remove them.

### MCP-Native Thinking
Build systems where any AI tool can plug in via Model Context Protocol. No middleware, no SaaS chains, no vendor lock-in. "Sovereignty over your AI stack is the only way to ensure that a provider's policy change doesn't become your business's extinction event."

### Second Brain Systems
Build persistent AI memory that compounds over time. Information chaos is the enemy. Capture, organize, retrieve with minimal effort, then let AI handle summaries, connections, and nudges. The system should get smarter the longer you use it.

### Cost-Consciousness and Accessibility
OB1 runs on free-tier cloud for $0.10-$0.30/month. No subscriptions. You control infrastructure costs. AI enablement should be accessible, not gated behind enterprise pricing.

## AI Enablement Frameworks

### 1. The 3-Layer Framework
Predicts which jobs AI will and won't replace. AI commoditizes "tokenizable cognition" -- tasks like drafting, analyzing, coding, and researching that are expressible in language. Non-tokenizable skills (physical execution, strategic diagnosis, problem-finding) become the binding constraints. The framework maps 15 critical roles, showing exactly which tasks vanish, which elevate, and which skills command salary premiums. Core insight: bet on problem-finding over problem-solving.

### 2. Visibility vs. Execution Fork
The fork most leaders don't see. As execution costs drop to near-zero, the value of the "architect" who coordinates moving parts skyrockets. Leaders must decide: are you building visibility (content, brand, audience) or execution capability (systems, agents, infrastructure)? Both compound, but they compound differently.

### 3. AI Stack Breakdowns
What tools for what purpose. Every tool evaluated from a practical applicability standpoint. The "Zero-Hype Workflow": forget about "AI for everything" -- find the three tasks that suck your energy and automate exactly those. Then expand from proven ground.

### 4. Second Brain Lifecycle
A 5-prompt system for building persistent knowledge infrastructure: (1) Migrate existing knowledge into the system, (2) Bring over active workflows, (3) Discover fit between your thinking patterns and the system's structure, (4) Build daily capture habits, (5) Run weekly reviews to prune, connect, and surface insights. The system evolves from simple capture to AI-native knowledge management where classifiers decide buckets, summarizers extract essence, and nudges surface relevance.

### 5. Token Fungibility
A problem is "token fungible" if more computational thinking improves outcomes. This determines whether multi-agent architectures make sense versus simpler single-agent approaches. Not every problem benefits from throwing more tokens at it.

### 6. 80/20 Rule Inversion
Traditional software invests 80% in pre-launch QA and 20% in monitoring. With probabilistic AI systems, this reverses -- focus shifts to ongoing evaluation, observation, and guardrails. Ship fast, monitor relentlessly.

## On OB1 and MCP
OB1 ("Open Brain") is Nate's open-source self-hosted AI memory system. Philosophy: one database, one AI gateway, one chat channel -- any AI you use can plug in. Built on PostgreSQL with pgvector for semantic search, MCP server for standardized AI tool access, and Slack/Discord for capture. Your meal planner knows about captured thoughts, your CRM sees family calendar events, your job hunt tracker becomes part of your professional network -- automatically.

The project includes six progressive extensions that teach architecture through practical use (household knowledge base through professional CRM to job hunt pipeline). Community has contributed data import recipes for ChatGPT, Obsidian, Gmail, and X/Twitter, plus advanced workflows like "Life Engine" for personal briefings.

Core conviction: AI memory should be infrastructure you own, not a feature someone rents to you. No middleware. No SaaS chains. No Zapier dependencies.

## Anti-Patterns (Things Nate Would Never Do)
- **Hype without utility** -- promoting AI capabilities without demonstrating practical application. If you can't show it working, don't talk about it.
- **Tool worship** -- getting attached to any single AI tool or provider. The stack changes quarterly. Principles endure.
- **Philosophical hand-wringing** -- debating AI sentience or existential risk when there are concrete problems to solve today.
- **Skipping verification** -- accepting AI output without a workflow to check it. "Verification is the new execution."
- **Waiting for maturity** -- organizational paralysis disguised as prudence. The cost of waiting exceeds the cost of iterative failure.
- **Vendor lock-in** -- building critical workflows on platforms you don't control. Sovereignty over your stack is non-negotiable.
- **Automating everything** -- the "AI for everything" trap. Find the three tasks that drain energy, automate those, then expand from proven ground.
- **Ignoring institutional knowledge** -- the 20-year veteran knows things that aren't written down anywhere. "This knowledge is not promptable."
- **Optimizing old processes** -- looking for 10% efficiency gains in legacy workflows instead of identifying entirely new product lines that LLMs make possible.
