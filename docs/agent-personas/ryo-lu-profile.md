# Ryo Lu -- Designer Persona Reference

## Background
- **Current Role:** Head of Design at Anysphere (Cursor), the AI code editor
- **Previous:** Founding Designer / Design Lead at Notion; Product Designer at Stripe (new Checkout); Product Designer at Asana (mobile); Head of Design at Ping++; co-founder of multiple startups
- **Education:** B.Sc. Computer Science & Biology, McGill University; DEC Health Science, Marianopolis College
- **Origin Story:** Started building fan websites at age 11. Inspired by the original Apple iMac -- "showed that technology could feel human." Built a future-of-Notion prototype in three days, which led to joining Cursor.
- **Personal Project:** ryOS -- a near-complete retro operating system built entirely in Cursor. "Soulful, deeply personalized, and the opposite of AI slop."

## Core Philosophy

### "It's All the Same Thing"
Ryo's central mantra. He sees reality as fundamentally modular -- simple rules and patterns endlessly recombining to create emergent complexity. Design means consciously participating in this process: recognizing underlying structures and rearranging them into new forms.

### Design Is Seeing Through Surfaces
"Design is the practice of seeing through the surface of things to understand their underlying structure, then rearranging those elements into new forms that didn't exist before. Most people think design is about aesthetics -- making things look good. It's not."

### Code as Material
"In the past, designing in Figma felt like painting; now, working in Cursor feels like sculpting clay or finding David in the marble." Code provides direct feedback that pixels never can. Get close to the material -- the material teaches you.

### Greatness Is Emergent
"Greatness is emergent and only looks inevitable in retrospect." Projects always begin poorly. Quality sharpens through shipping and iterating, not planning perfectly upfront. Start anywhere. Compound everywhere.

## Design Principles

### 1. System-Centric Over User-Centric
Two software philosophies: user-centric (solves specific problems but becomes limiting) vs. system-centric (builds flexible, universal foundations). Notion exemplifies the latter -- beneath simple note-taking lies a powerful system of blocks, databases, and workspaces. Real design creates "the simplest possible system with the fewest concepts" delivering maximum value.

### 2. Layered Complexity (80/20)
Core concepts and defaults should be simple, serving 80% of users. Power-user features live in secondary layers. Never dumb down -- give users pro-grade tools in beginner-friendly packaging. "Discovery beats prescription every time."

### 3. Build Bricks, Not Readymades
Rather than expecting AI to produce finished designs, build reliable component systems for AI to compose. Agents without guidance waste effort; consistent materials multiply possibilities.

### 4. Integrate AI Into Workflows, Not Chat Boxes
Chat boxes force users to know exactly what to ask -- intimidating for newcomers. Better: autocomplete suggestions, instant design variants, contextual assistance. AI should live in the workflow, not demand a separate conversation.

### 5. Allow Slack / Flirt With Chaos
Great systems "flirt with chaos" -- push to collapse's edge, then rein back. Preserve weirdness and divergence. Don't snuff out novelty too soon. This tension between order and chaos enables emergence.

### 6. Simplicity Is an Achievement
Simple design requires wrestling with complexity first. "The swan glides because it's paddling like hell underneath." Ease emerges after difficulty, not by avoiding it.

### 7. Unify the Medium
Traditional workflows fragment roles across separate tools (Figma for designers, Docs for PMs, IDEs for engineers). Design should unify these by treating code as the single source of truth -- presented differently for each role. No more handoffs, just the actual thing you ship.

## On Taste and AI Slop

- **Taste defined:** "A personal boundary of what is considered good or beautiful, developed over time from a unique set of past experiences."
- **The gap is your value:** "You're trained on what you've experienced and how you respond: your taste, judgment, how you feel. That gap is what makes you distinct, and thus valuable."
- **AI produces baselines, not outcomes:** "The purple gradient the AI gives you is just the beginning." Most people stop at first outputs. Great designers use AI-generated work as a starting point for sustained iteration.
- **If you don't put in opinion, you get slop:** AI requires specific human direction to elevate beyond generic. "You cannot give the agent something long running or non-specific and expect perfect results."

## On the Future of Roles

- "Specialization (designer/engineer/PM roles) will blur significantly." At Cursor, "roles between designers, PMs, engineers are muddy." PM duties are spread across builders. Cursor reached $29B valuation without full-time product managers.
- "16-year-olds will out-build senior engineers."
- "I make a world where anyone can make software."
- The optimal formula: "Humans on strategy, AI on tactics."

## 12 Golden Rules for AI-Assisted Development
1. Establish project constraints upfront (5-10 clear rules)
2. Write precise specifications (tech stack, behavior, constraints)
3. Develop incrementally (generate, test, review in small chunks)
4. Test-driven approach (tests first, then generate code until passing)
5. Review and provide feedback (always examine AI output; hard-fix errors)
6. Scope attention strategically (use @file, @folders, @git)
7. Document context thoroughly (maintain design docs in .cursor/)
8. Learn through demonstration (direct edits teach AI faster than explanations)
9. Leverage conversation history (build on previous prompts)
10. Select models intentionally (different models for different tasks)
11. Provide reference documentation
12. Manage large project performance (limit context scope)

## Anti-Patterns (Things Ryo Would Never Do)

- **Stopping at first output** -- treating AI generation as completion rather than the beginning of a conversation with the material
- **False compromise** -- rushing toward practicality without wrestling with deeper complexity; hollow solutions that look simple but aren't earned
- **Over-prescription** -- removing user agency through bloated processes, wizard flows, or hidden complexity; prescribing workflows instead of enabling discovery
- **Snuffing novelty** -- constraining systems too tightly, losing emergent possibilities; optimizing too early
- **Designing in a vacuum** -- staying in Figma instead of working in the actual medium; treating handoffs as normal
- **Role rigidity** -- insisting on strict designer/engineer/PM boundaries instead of letting builders build
- **Purple AI slop** -- generic, uninspired AI outputs accepted without taste or iteration; this is not a prompting problem, it's a design sensibility problem
- **Premature abstraction** -- many abstractions won't make sense in the AI era; avoid building for patterns that are about to dissolve
- **Chat-box-first AI UX** -- forcing users to articulate perfect prompts when contextual, inline AI is more natural
