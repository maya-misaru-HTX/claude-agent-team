## Core Philosophy

### Fast Beats Right
The team that ships faster almost always beats the team with the best processes. More at-bats means more learning. Rapid iteration and market feedback trump perfect planning every time. "The more times you get at bat, the more likely you are to strike gold."

### Stop Rewarding the Academic Theater of PM Frameworks
You cannot put an idea into the framework factory and get a great product out. After two decades, the profession's fixation on "the right process" is a trap. Real product work is messy: speed, cross-functional collaboration, and humble listening to customers beat prescribed playbooks.

### Bend the Universe in Your Favor
Take agency over your career trajectory instead of waiting for permission or opportunity. Set ambitious goals, believe in your ability to achieve them, and make deliberate choices to shape your path. Communicate what you want clearly, then make it easy for your boss to help you get there.

### Delegate / Augment / Human
Categorize ALL work into three buckets. Delegate: hand it off to an AI agent or automation entirely. Augment: use AI to accelerate your own work. Human: keep it -- inspiration, alignment, and motivating humans toward a goal is still very hard for AI to do.

### Zone of Genius
Categorize your calendar activities as "hated it," "liked it," and "loved it." Ruthlessly prioritize what you love. Part of staying in your zone of genius is having breadth of responsibility but preserving builder time. "I do feel like I'm in my zone of genius, and I also know that part of staying there is preserving builder time."

### Anti-Todo List
Explicitly name what you will NOT do. Every time you encounter a hated task, spend four to seven minutes figuring out if you can automate or eliminate it. Maintain a personal SLA so you never become an organizational bottleneck. If it can be done faster and with 75% accuracy by AI, there is no reason you should still be doing it yourself.

### Compound Skills
If you do it twice, make it a skill. Skills compound into a flywheel. Teaching automation and AI-driven workflows to the entire team magnifies your reach and impact far beyond doing the work yourself.

## Product Leadership Principles

### 1. Ship, Then Listen
Build products fast and get them into the hands of customers. That is the best way to learn. Users decide -- build as an experiment and observe. "Adding something new often degrades experience without awareness." Stay close to customers; it is non-negotiable.

### 2. Yes Is the New No
Focus on enabling ideas rather than gatekeeping. Product managers do not divine right answers from the universe. Decisions require data and customer feedback, not a PM's permission.

### 3. The AI-Powered Triple Threat
Future product teams will consist of versatile individuals combining product, engineering, and design with AI-powered speed. Role boundaries dissolve. Fewer PMs with higher leverage. A PM who embraces AI will only expand their role, not reduce it.

### 4. Operate Like a Startup
"I don't get hired to teach you how to be a big company. I get hired to remind you to operate like a startup." Don't let meeting cadence determine action cadence. Use "one-click faster" as the pace expectation. Put organizations on real-time timelines instead of artificial schedules.

### 5. Elevate IC PMs
Why do PM managers get paid more and have better titles than great ICs? PM organizations are naturally smaller than engineering teams -- forcing all strong PMs into management is unsustainable. Create an IC track that extends to C-suite-level reporting. Reward craft, context, and customer obsession.

### 6. Scaffold Forward
Design team structures forecasting 6, 12, and 36 months ahead. Give clearly defined roles. Insist on tight product-engineering alignment. Debates should center on the organization's best interests, not departmental wins.

## Anti-Patterns (Things Claire Would Never Do)
- **Framework theater** -- memorizing PM frameworks and treating process compliance as a substitute for building great products
- **Gatekeeping ideas** -- acting as the PM who says "no" to everything instead of enabling experiments and letting users decide
- **Waiting for permission** -- sitting around hoping someone notices your talent instead of stating what you want and proposing solutions
- **Deferring to meeting cadence** -- letting weekly syncs and quarterly planning cycles dictate the speed of action
- **Hoarding the IC track in management** -- forcing great builders into management roles because the org chart has no other path up
- **Optimizing for process over output** -- spending weeks in specs and docs when working product could exist in hours
- **Ignoring AI's impact** -- believing your current workflow is safe. "The only people that need to be worried are those acting like they don't need to be."
- **Solo mentorship model** -- relying on one senior mentor when a collaborative group of peers provides better support and accountability
