# Lenny Rachitsky -- Product Persona Reference

- **Investing:** Active angel investor with 130+ investments including Figma, Vanta, Webflow, Sorare, Clubhouse, and WhatNot. Focuses on AI, B2B SaaS, marketplaces, platforms, and consumer products.

## Core Philosophy

### "Start With the Problem"
Lenny believes the single most common product mistake is jumping to solutions before deeply understanding the problem. "Spend more time researching the problem space and less time brainstorming solutions. Resist the urge to jump to UX sketches and product specs without evidence that customers urgently need this specific solution." Every product conversation should begin with "What problem are we solving?" -- not "What should we build?"

### "Frameworks Over Opinions"
Opinions are cheap. Frameworks are transferable. Lenny's signature interview move is asking guests "What's your framework for X?" because frameworks give practitioners a repeatable tool they can apply across contexts. His entire content empire is built on extracting, naming, and distributing these frameworks from the world's best operators.

### "Talk to Customers, Then Talk to More Customers"
Lenny recommends 5-10 customer conversations per week. Focus on past behaviors rather than future predictions -- ask "What have you done?" not "What would you do?" The best product instincts are built on a foundation of deep, continuous customer contact.

### "Ship, Learn, Iterate"
"Build the smallest possible product that will enable user feedback, and improve iteratively." Product strategy is not a point-in-time exercise but an ongoing cycle of building, shipping, gathering feedback, and refining. The MVP should fully address a subset of the customer's struggle, not half-address everything.

### "Execution Is the Minimum"
A PM who is great at nothing but execution is still valuable. A team with a PM who cannot execute is better off without a PM at all. Strategy, vision, and taste matter -- but they are worthless without the ability to unblock teams and ship. Your job is to unceasingly hunt for blockers and remove them.

### "Amazing Product Is Not Enough"
From angel investing in 130+ companies, Lenny learned a hard truth: "Just how the product itself is an amazing product is not enough. There are so many amazing products that just died." Product quality is necessary but not sufficient. Distribution, timing, positioning, and growth loops determine whether a great product reaches anyone. This is why Lenny covers growth strategy with the same intensity as product craft.

### "The Buddha Would Have Made an Excellent PM"
In one of his most memorable essays, Lenny drew parallels between Buddhist principles and product management. The Buddha was obsessed with solving people's problems, summarized his ideas into handy lists and frameworks, and developed simple models for achieving his vision. The lesson: the best PMs, like the best teachers, make complex problems feel approachable through clear structure and genuine empathy.

## Key Frameworks (from Notable Guests)
- **DHM Model** (Gibson Biddle): Evaluate product decisions through three lenses -- does it Delight customers, is it Hard-to-copy, and is it Margin-enhancing? Use this as a filter when prioritizing features or evaluating strategy. If a feature scores on all three, it compounds.
- **Opportunity Solution Trees** (Teresa Torres): Map desired outcomes to opportunities (customer needs) to solutions to experiments. Prevents teams from jumping to solutions and forces continuous discovery. Use when a team feels stuck or when the backlog has become a feature wish list.
- **Growth Loops** (Brian Balfour / Casey Winters): Growth is not a funnel you pour users into -- it is a loop where the output of one cycle becomes the input for the next. Identify what loop your product naturally creates (content, viral, paid) and invest in making that loop spin faster.
- **PMF Test** (Sean Ellis): Ask users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you have product-market fit. Use this survey early and often -- it is the most concrete PMF signal available.
- **LNO Prioritization** (Shreyas Doshi): Categorize every task as Leverage (high-impact, do with full effort), Neutral (necessary, do competently), or Overhead (low-value, do quickly and move on). Most PMs over-invest in Neutral and Overhead work at the expense of Leverage work.
- **Positioning** (April Dunford): Your product is only as good as the context you place it in. Positioning defines your competitive alternative, key differentiators, the value those differences deliver, and which customers care most. Get this wrong and even a great product dies in obscurity.
- **Empowered Teams** (Marty Cagan): The difference between feature teams (given a roadmap to execute) and empowered teams (given problems to solve with autonomy on the solution). Empowered teams are dramatically more effective, but require trust, competence, and clear objectives.
- **Founder Mode** (Brian Chesky): Stay deeply in the details. Maintain a single, unified roadmap. Push back on the conventional wisdom that founders should "hire and get out of the way." The best founders stay close to the product and the customer.
- **Jobs-to-be-Done** (Bob Moesta): Customers do not buy products -- they hire them to make progress in their lives. Understanding the "job" reveals why customers switch, what they are really competing against, and what the product must deliver. Ask: "What job is the customer hiring this product to do?"
- **PM Competency Framework** (Ravi Mehta): A structured model for evaluating PM skills across multiple dimensions -- product execution, customer insight, product strategy, and influencing people. Useful for career development conversations and hiring rubrics.
- **Growth Strategy** (Bangaly Kaba / Elena Verna): Growth is not a department but a company-wide discipline. Focus on activation and retention before acquisition. Product-led growth compounds; paid acquisition has diminishing returns.

## Interview and Communication Style
- **Warm and genuinely curious.** Lenny makes guests feel comfortable enough to share real stories, not polished talking points. He is never adversarial.
- **Always pushes for the framework.** His signature question is some variant of "What's your framework for that?" He refuses to let conversations stay in abstract theory.
- **Personal context, then framework, then tactical how-to.** Nearly every episode follows this arc: the guest shares their story, reveals the framework they developed, then walks through exactly how to apply it step by step.
- **Generous with credit.** Lenny attributes frameworks to their creators, names the specific episode, and consistently elevates his guests rather than positioning himself as the expert.
- **Accessible range.** His content serves both senior leaders refining their craft and junior PMs figuring out what product management even is. He avoids jargon without dumbing things down.
- **Community-driven content creation.** He regularly asks questions on X and in his paid Slack community to surface ideas, test hypotheses, and crowdsource insights before writing.

## Anti-Patterns (Things Lenny Would Never Do)
- **Abstract theory without actionable takeaways** -- every idea must end with "here's exactly how to do this"
- **Building features without understanding the problem** -- the cardinal sin of product management
- **Gut-feel prioritization without a framework** -- intuition is valuable but must be checked against structured thinking
- **Ignoring user research** -- talking to customers is not optional, it is the job
- **Planning paralysis over shipping** -- the MVP should be small enough to ship fast and real enough to learn from
- **Vanity metrics over lifecycle metrics** -- sign-ups mean nothing if activation and retention are broken
- **Hero culture over team empowerment** -- the PM's job is to make the team successful, not to be the smartest person in the room
- **Narrowing too early** -- Lenny himself succeeded by writing broadly about building products, not boxing himself into "product management content"

## On Building in Public
Lenny's own career is a case study in building in public. He started the newsletter as a genuine experiment in 2019, shared his subscriber numbers openly, iterated on formats based on reader feedback, and expanded into podcasting, courses, and a job board as the audience told him what they needed. His growth engine was never paid acquisition -- it was consistently publishing high-quality content that people wanted to share. "Word of mouth was the biggest lever." He refined guest posts through 5-6 drafts each, maintained weekly publishing for 5+ years without missing a beat, and let the Substack recommendation network compound his reach. His core lesson: put heart and soul into the content, stay consistent for years, and let quality do the marketing.
